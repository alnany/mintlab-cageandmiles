What Is a High Net-Worth Divorce?

High net-worth divorces are divorces where significant wealth or complex high-value assets are part of the marital estate. While there’s no single legal definition, these cases often include:

  • Business ownership or equity holdings
  • Hedge funds and venture capital interests
  • Extensive investment and securities portfolios
  • Multiple real estate holdings (personal and/or commercial)
  • Trusts, complex estate planning, and family offices
  • Intellectual property or entertainment revenue
  • Executive compensation, deferred income, and stock options

These cases are more intricate than standard divorces and require advanced planning, financial analysis, and strategic negotiation or litigation.

Preparing for a High Net-Worth Divorce in California

Disclosure is the first substantial step in any divorce process. Each party is required to provide the other with all material facts and information regarding all assets, debts, income, and expenses.

In a high net worth divorce, the disclosure process can be lengthy and complex. In preparing for a high asset divorce, you may consider reaching out to financial professionals such as business advisors, CPAs, financial advisors, investment teams, real estate professionals, family estate planning attorneys and anyone else who might have pertinent information or documentation regarding your estate. Beginning to gather documents and information will give you a head start and help the divorce process move along more quickly and efficiently.

Unique Issues in High Net-Worth Divorces

The intricacies of a high net-worth estate bring certain challenges to the divorce process. Some unique issues that high net worth couples face during divorce:

  • Tax considerations when transferring assets
  • Family estate planning for the benefit of the children of the marriage
  • Separate property trusts and inheritance rights
  • Premarital agreement disputes
  • Hidden assets or failure to disclose assets
  • Spousal support considering the marital standard of living
  • Forensic accounting to determine complex financial issues such as income available for support or tracing of separate property assets to preserve their separate character
  • Business valuations or other complex valuations

Our team of high asset divorce attorneys work with trusted financial experts to help us accurately value your marital assets and equitably divide them in a way that is fair to both parties.

Frequently Asked Questions

What qualifies as a high-net-worth divorce?

A high-net-worth divorce generally involves substantial or complex assets that require advanced financial analysis and strategic planning. These cases often include business ownership, investment and retirement portfolios, multiple real estate properties, executive compensation, venture capital firms, hedge funds, trusts, complex estate plans, or intellectual property. While there is no specific dollar threshold under California law, the complexity of the assets involved typically defines a high-asset divorce.

How are assets valued in a high-net-worth divorce?

Assets in a high-net-worth divorce are commonly valued using financial experts such as tax professionals, forensic accountants, business valuation professionals, and investment specialists. These experts help determine current market value, future earning potential, tax implications, and whether an asset is community or separate property, ensuring a fair and accurate division.

Can a high-net-worth divorce be resolved without litigation?

Yes, many high-net-worth divorces are often resolved through negotiation, mediation, or collaborative divorce rather than litigation. However, because these cases often involve complex financial structures and significant assets, having an experienced high-net-worth divorce attorney is essential to protect your interests and ensure any settlement is both equitable and enforceable.

What are the steps to identify, value, and divide assets in a high-net-worth divorce?

High-net-worth families often own complex assets that require specialized valuation and division—privately held business interests, commercial real estate, or restricted stock options and deferred compensation. The process involves three key steps:

  1. Conduct a detailed asset review with your attorney to identify what needs valuation and whether you'll need independent valuation experts. This often includes separate property assets you might not expect to have to value.
  2. Determine your preferred division strategy, i.e., which assets are best for your future. Even assets with identical fair market values can have very different benefits for you—keeping one over another could significantly impact your financial future.
  3. Effectuate your division strategy. This is especially challenging with non-transferrable business interests or lopsided portfolios that are logistically difficult to split, requiring creative and customized solutions.

How do complex financial portfolios affect high-net-worth cases?

Complex portfolios create both challenges and opportunities. Skilled counsel can turn this complexity into leverage, maximizing benefits that wouldn't exist in straightforward divorces.

How is income treated differently in high-net-worth divorces?

High-net-worth families often have sources of non-wage income such as trust payments, rental properties, K-1 distributions, or business expense accounts. The key question: do these count as income for child support and spousal support calculations?

Courts typically include payments and benefits that reasonably resemble income when calculating support. This determination includes a lot of discretion and depends on factors like frequency and reliability. If a trust or business pays your living expenses, car costs, meals, or provides a credit card, those benefits might be treated as income. How you frame and argue these monetary benefits can determine whether the court considers them income available for support.

For W-2 wage earners, tax returns are presumptively correct as income. For earners with other sources of income, tax returns are just the start of the analysis. Certain items like tax deductible expenses or depreciation might be added back to income for support purposes. Be prepared to explain your income and expenses in detail.

A related concept is marital standard of living (MSOL), which establishes what the court views as reasonable needs. MSOL determinations are highly contentious—the spouse requesting support often inflates it, while the paying spouse deflates it. This becomes particularly complex in high-net-worth divorces where families live beyond conventional American means, making it difficult to establish what's "reasonable." Overreaching in either direction will lose you credibility quickly.
